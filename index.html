    Ph.D. Research: Abstract & Key Contriubutions
    Thesis title: Energy Usage and Tire Usage in Electric Vehicles with In-Wheel Motors.

    Abstract:   Electric vehicles (EVs) with in-wheel motors (IWMs) are a rapidly emerging technology. These EVs lie in a wide power interval, are of different types belonging to the categories of bikes, microcars, mid-size cars, full-size cars, and buses, and use one or multiple IWMs. Additionally, IWMs enable a wide range of vehicle configurations, free up space inside the EV, obviate the need for mechanical transmission and axles, and enable the EV to have modular designs. A modular design is often a wheel assembly primarily consisting of an IWM, a steering motor, suspension, and brake. Among the many recent advancements in EV technologies, the modular designs make overactuated EVs possible and attractive. For example, a set of four modular designs can help build a four-wheel independent steering four-wheel independent drive (4WIS4WID) EV; this EV has received attention from the industry and academia, especially in the last two decades. As the EVs with IWMs 1) lie in a wide power interval, 2) are of different types, 3) use one or multiple IWMs, and 4) can help build overactuated EVs that have the potential to achieve a condition known as optimal tire usage (OTU), there is a need to investigate 1) these EVs from energy usage perspective and 2) overacutated EVs from tire usage perspective. This thesis explores EVs with IWMs from these two perspectives.
      Towards the energy usage perspective, this thesis considers battery-powered EVs (BEVs), lying in the power interval of [0.25 -- 360] kW and groups them into five classes. The energy usage of a BEV is a determinant of its operational cost and environmental impact. A graph of energy efficiency versus power is important while investing in new BEV technologies. In constructing this graph over the said power interval for single motor drive (SMD) BEVs, the existing literature on energy efficiency provides some data for two of the five classes, while for IWM BEVs, the literature provides some data for just one value of power. To construct these graphs for both SMD and IWM BEVs, this thesis performs a comprehensive study of each class of BEV to gather realistic data for each stage involved in the calculation of their energy efficiencies. It concludes that the tank-to-wheel energy efficiency of an IWM BEV may be up to 18\% higher than that of an SMD BEV of the same power. In examining how efficient the BEVs are with respect to internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles, this thesis concludes that the well-to-wheels energy efficiencies of IWM BEVs, SMD BEVs, and ICE vehicles, each of around 80 kW, are about 33\%, 30\%, and 18\%, respectively for natural gas pathway.
      Towards the tire usage perspective, this thesis primarily considers 4WIS4WID EVs, which are widely studied and are popular among overactuated EVs. These EVs have an independent steering motor and an independent driving motor for each wheel, for a total of eight motors. About fifteen works in this emerging field have shown partial or full path-tracking control (PTC) algorithms for these EVs. Of the fifteen works, ten explicitly or implicitly aspire for the condition known as OTU; the PTC algorithms are additionally responsible for this condition. This condition helps achieve 1) a superior vehicle stability margin that improves the vehicle's stability, controllability, responsiveness, and cornering behavior, and 2) some improvement in energy efficiency, in comparison to conventional EVs. Seven of the ten works have shown full PTC algorithms for these EVs. This thesis performs in-depth block-diagrammatic examinations (BDEs) of five works among the seven to understand a PTC algorithm deeply, and to identify modifications, if any, needed for OTU.
     The thesis finds that while these five works have performed suitable control allocation for OTU, examining whether their motor control systems (MCSs) are appropriate for fulfilling the control allocation demands, is required. In particular, the BDEs reveal that a) two of the five works have not paid attention to MCSs, b) the third work needs modification in the driving MCSs, and c) the fourth and fifth work seems to have appropriate MCSs for OTU. The MCSs of the fifth work use a disturbance observer-based control (DOBC) scheme, and seem like a good candidate for MCSs in the first three works. Thus this thesis a) considers the MCSs of the fifth work, b) makes appropriate modifications to them, c) performs the effectiveness analyses of the MCSs and their modified versions, and d) therefrom recommends necessary MCSs for the first three works. Further, this thesis also examines the effectiveness of a widely used and popular scheme, known as active disturbance rejection control (ADRC), as an alternative to the DOBC scheme for the third and fifth works. The PTC algorithm of the fifth work employs these two schemes to demonstrate the achievement of OTU in simulation. It shows that these schemes are sufficient (in simulation) for OTU in the fifth work, and are likely to be sufficient for all PTC algorithms that perform the control allocation required for OTU.
    Key Contributions:
    • A generalized Energy Flow Diagram (EFD) for calculating Tank-to-Wheel (TTW) energy efficiency for all types of BEVs.

    • A control-system-theoretic block diagram of the EFD, along with the derivation of an equation to compute the TTW energy efficiency of BEVs.

    • An extensive literature survey to find, estimate, or assume efficiencies for the eight stages in the energy flow diagram across five types of BEVs.

    • A graph of TTW energy efficiency versus electric vehicle power.

    • Calculation of TTW energy efficiency for arbitrary four-wheeled BEVs.

    • A brief commentary on the TTW energy efficiency of a 4WIS4WID BEV.

    • Recommendations for improving the TTW energy efficiency of BEVs.

    • A brief commentary on:
      1. Qualitative trends in TTW energy efficiency of BEVs,

      2. Incorporating margins within TTW energy efficiency, and

      3. Correctness of the developed results.

    • Energy Flow Diagrams (EFDs) for calculating Well-to-Wheel (WTW) energy efficiency of IWM BEVs, SMD BEVs, and ICE vehicles — all full-sized cars — for the natural gas pathway.

    • A discussion on the meaning and importance of Optimal Tire Usage (OTU).

    • Presentation of three OTU indicators that estimate OTU in the KA works.

    • A pictorial comparison of FWD, RWD, 4WID, and 4WIS4WID EVs based on OTU.

    • Block-diagrammatic examination of five PTC algorithms of 4WIS4WID EVs for OTU.

    • Block-diagrammatic representation of vehicle units using Pacejka and Burckhardt tire models.

    • Discussion on the importance of drawing block diagrams for PTC algorithms.

    • A brief comparison of the five referenced works.

    • Identification of required modifications for OTU in the five referenced works.

    • A brief commentary on block-diagrammatic linear models of DC motors and IWMs.

    • Examination of the effectiveness of Disturbance Observer-Based Control (DOBC) and Active Disturbance Rejection Control (ADRC) schemes using a speed-dependent disturbance model under parametric uncertainty.

    • Comparison of the DOBC and ADRC schemes for speed tracking of driving wheels.

    • Recommendation of the DOBC and ADRC schemes for the driving motor control system (MCS) of one FCA work.

    • Recommendation of two suitable DOBC structures for load-torque tracking MCSs of the remaining two FCA works.

    • Employment of HGC, DOBC, and ADRC schemes in one PTC algorithm to examine OTU, demonstrated via simulation using MATLAB/Simulink.
